About Me

I am an economics Ph.D. Candidate in the Department of Economics at Indiana University Bloomington. My primary research fields are in Industrial Organization and Networks; however, I also have interests in Experimental Economics. My dissertation studies the unintended consequences of Airbnb and its role as a catalyst for tourism gentrification. I received my B.A. in Economics and Mathematics from Wabash College in 2013, my M.A. in Economics from Indiana University in 2015, and expect to complete my Ph.D. by May 2019.

I am on the job market this year and will be available for interviews at the AEA/ASSA annual meeting in Atlanta in January, 2019.

Working Papers

Inequality vs Altruism: Experimental Evidence

Abstract: The literature on dictator games has long debated whether inequality aversion or altruism was the motivation behind giving, but generally assumed the two preferences were mutually exclusive. This paper proposes an alternative theory suggesting subjects can express both altruism and inequality aversion. To test this theory a novel version of a three-player dictator game is introduced. The dictator chooses how to distribute a fixed endowment between two recipients, and is able to earn a private return based on the level of inequality resulting from the allocation decision. To ensure inequality averse preferences can be separately identified from self-regarding behavior the domain of decision environments is restricted to those in which the dictator receives the highest payout. Results show there exists a subset of subjects that express behavior inline with both altruism and inequality aversion. Furthermore, the results suggest the social preferences displayed in the experiment can be influenced by the order of decision environments. The implications of these findings stress the importance of accounting for interactions between social preferences as well as a counterbalanced experimental design.
